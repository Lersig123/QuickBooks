Welcome — You've got this

A friendly 2-week path from "What's QuickBooks?" to "I can run the daily bookkeeping for my client."

How this dashboard works

This guide takes about 1 to 2 weeks if you spend roughly 45 minutes a day on it. Each lesson lives on the left sidebar. Click through them in order. When you finish a lesson, hit the green Mark Complete button at the bottom — you'll see your progress fill the bar at the top.

Your progress is saved automatically in this browser. If you reset, it clears everything.

At the end of two weeks, you will be able to honestly say: "Yes, I know my way around QuickBooks." You will not be a CPA. You will not file taxes. But you'll be able to do the day-to-day work that most clients hire VAs for: sending invoices, recording payments, tracking expenses, and pulling simple reports.

One promise back from you

Rule #1: When you're not sure where something goes, ASK your client or their accountant. Bookkeeping is forgiving when you communicate. It is unforgiving when you guess.
Week 1 · Foundations · Day 1

What a VA Actually Needs to Know

Essential vs. "you can learn this later." Knowing the difference saves you weeks.

Objective

Understand what QuickBooks is in plain English.

Goal

Be able to tell a friend what your client uses it for.

Time

~30 minutes

What is QuickBooks?

QuickBooks Online (often shortened to QBO) is a website that small businesses use to keep track of money. That's it. It records two main things: money coming in (someone paid them) and money going out (they paid for something). Everything else is just organizing those two things into pretty reports.

Think of it like the food diary of a business. Instead of writing down what you ate, you write down every dollar that moved.

Why your client hired you to use it

Business owners hate doing this. They are good at selling cookies or styling hair or coaching CEOs. They are usually not good at, or interested in, typing receipts into a computer. That's where you come in. You handle the boring-but-important typing so they can focus on the work that earns the money.

What you NEED to know — the essential 8

If you can do these eight things, 90% of clients are thrilled with you:

  1. Essential   Log in and find your way around the dashboard
  2. Essential   Create and send an invoice to a customer
  3. Essential   Record a payment when a customer pays
  4. Essential   Issue a sales receipt for paid-on-the-spot sales
  5. Essential   Record an expense (something the business bought)
  6. Essential   Enter a bill that the business owes
  7. Essential   Categorize new bank transactions in the bank feed
  8. Essential   Run a Profit & Loss report and a Balance Sheet

What you can learn LATER (don't worry about these yet)

  • Later   Payroll — usually a CPA or payroll specialist handles it.
  • Later   Sales tax filing — leave this to the accountant.
  • Later   Journal entries — almost never something a beginner VA should touch.
  • Later   Bank reconciliation (the formal monthly close) — observe at first.
  • Later   Setting up a new company file — your client already has one.
  • Later   Inventory tracking — only if the business sells physical products.
Translation: Your job is everyday data entry. Bigger decisions belong to the accountant. Stay in your lane and you'll be a star.

The three numbers your client actually cares about

When you understand these three, you understand 80% of what bookkeeping is for:

  • Revenue — total money the business brought in.
  • Expenses — total money the business spent.
  • Profit — what's left when you subtract expenses from revenue. Revenue minus Expenses = Profit.

Every single thing you do in QuickBooks ends up affecting one of these three numbers.

Customers, Vendors & the Chart of Accounts

Three lists that QuickBooks runs on. Master these and the rest is easy.

Objective

Understand the three master lists in QBO.

Goal

Never confuse a customer with a vendor again.

Time

~40 minutes

Customer vs Vendor — the simplest test in the world

CustomerVendor
Pays your client money 💰Receives money from your client 💸
Example: A buyer of servicesExample: The internet company
Lives under the "Customers" tabLives under the "Vendors" tab

That's it. Money flows IN from a customer. Money flows OUT to a vendor. If you remember nothing else from today, remember that arrow direction.

What's a Chart of Accounts?

The Chart of Accounts (or "COA" if someone's feeling fancy) is just a list of buckets. Every dollar moving in or out of the business has to go into one of these buckets.

Typical buckets a small business might have:

  • Sales (money in)
  • Consulting Income (money in)
  • Office Supplies (money out)
  • Rent (money out)
  • Internet & Phone (money out)
  • Meals & Entertainment (money out)
  • Software Subscriptions (money out)

When you record a $50 trip to Staples, you'd put that under "Office Supplies." When a customer pays $400 for consulting, that goes under "Consulting Income." You're sorting laundry into the right baskets.

Money Words, Explained Like You're 15

The 10 accounting words that scare beginners — translated.

Objective

De-mystify accounting vocabulary.

Goal

Hear any of these words without panicking.

Time

~40 minutes

The translation table

Fancy wordPlain English
Revenue / IncomeMoney the business earned.
ExpenseMoney the business spent.
AssetStuff the business owns (cash in the bank, a laptop, a delivery van).
LiabilityMoney the business owes (credit card balance, a loan).
EquityWhat's left for the owner after subtracting what they owe from what they own.
Accounts Receivable (A/R)Money customers owe to the business.
Accounts Payable (A/P)Money the business owes to vendors.
InvoiceA "please pay me" bill the business sends to a customer.
BillA "please pay me" bill the business receives from a vendor.
ReconcileComparing the bank statement to QuickBooks to make sure they match.

The two flows you'll do all day

Money coming in (sales flow):

  1. You create an invoice for the customer.
  2. The customer eventually pays.
  3. You record the payment in QuickBooks against that invoice.

Money going out (purchase flow):

  1. The vendor sends a bill (or your client buys something directly).
  2. Your client pays.
  3. You record the expense or bill payment in QuickBooks.

Invoice vs Sales Receipt — the difference

Both record a sale. The difference is timing:

InvoiceSales Receipt
Customer will pay laterCustomer pays right now
Use for B2B work, services, anything billedUse for retail, on-the-spot sales, deposits
Creates Accounts Receivable until paidGoes straight to income — done in one step
Memory trick: Invoice = "I'll pay later." Sales Receipt = "Here's your money now."

Expense vs Bill — the difference

ExpenseBill
Already paid — just recording itWill pay later — recording what's owed
Use when client swipes their cardUse when vendor sends an invoice to be paid in 30 days

Week 2 · Daily SOPs · Day 6

SOP 1 · Create an Invoice

The single most common task you'll do. Master this and you're 25% of the way to "useful VA."

Objective

Generate a professional invoice for a customer.

Goal

Send a clean invoice in under 3 minutes.

Description

You'll record what was sold, to whom, and email it out.

When to use this SOP

Use this any time your client tells you, "Please send an invoice to ___ for ___." If the customer will pay later (not on the spot), this is the right tool.

Before you start, you need

  • Customer name (already in QuickBooks? If not, add them first — SOP 6)
  • What was sold (the service or product)
  • How much to charge
  • Payment terms (usually "Net 30" — means due in 30 days)

Step-by-step

  1. Click + New in the top-left corner of QuickBooks.
  2. Under the "Customers" column, click Invoice. A blank invoice form opens.
  3. In the Customer field at the top, click the dropdown and pick your customer's name. (If they're not listed, save the invoice as a draft and add them via SOP 6 first.)
  4. Confirm the Invoice date is today. Set Terms to "Net 30" unless your client says otherwise. The Due date auto-fills.
  5. In the line items area, click Product/Service and pick the right item from the dropdown (e.g., "Consulting Hours," "Web Design Package").
  6. Type a clear Description — what the customer actually got. "Logo design — 3 concepts" is better than "design work."
  7. Enter Quantity and Rate. The amount calculates automatically.
  8. If there's more than one item, click Add lines and repeat.
  9. (Optional) Add a message in Message on invoice — something like "Thanks for your business!" — your client appreciates the warmth.
  10. Look at the total. Does it match what your client asked for? If yes, continue. If no, fix it.
  11. Click Save and send (bottom right). A preview pops up — confirm the customer's email is correct.
  12. Click Send and close. Done. The customer just got an invoice in their inbox.

Week 2 · Daily SOPs · Day 7

SOP 2 · Record a Payment

When the customer finally pays the invoice you sent, this is how you close the loop.

Objective

Mark an invoice as paid in QuickBooks.

Goal

Apply payment to the correct invoice every time.

Description

Match incoming money to an open invoice.

When to use this SOP

Use this whenever a customer pays an existing invoice. The money might come in via check, bank transfer, Stripe, PayPal, or cash. Doesn't matter how — if there's an invoice waiting, you "receive payment" against it.

Important: Do NOT just create a sales receipt or a deposit. That would double-count the income. The invoice already counts the sale. Always use "Receive payment."

Before you start, you need

  • The customer's name
  • How much they paid (exact amount)
  • The date they paid
  • How they paid (check #, bank deposit, credit card, etc.)

Step-by-step

  1. Click + New.
  2. Under "Customers," click Receive payment.
  3. In the Customer dropdown, pick the customer who paid. Their open invoices appear below.
  4. Set the Payment date to the actual date the money was received.
  5. Pick the Payment method (Check, Credit card, Bank transfer, etc.).
  6. If they paid by check, type the Reference no. (the check number).
  7. In Deposit to, pick the bank account the money landed in (usually "Checking" or "Undeposited Funds" — ask your client which one they use).
  8. In the invoices list below, check the box next to the invoice they're paying. The amount usually fills in automatically.
  9. Confirm the Amount received at the top matches what they actually paid.
  10. Click Save and close. The invoice is now marked PAID.

Customer: Beta Co.
Payment date: 05/16/2026   Method: Bank transfer
Deposit to: Checking
OPEN INVOICES (check the one being paid)

SOP 3 · Issue a Sales Receipt

For when the customer pays on the spot — a one-step combo of "invoice + payment."

Objective

Record a paid-on-the-spot sale.

Goal

Email a clean receipt to the customer.

Description

Use this for retail, walk-in, or instant-pay sales.

When to use this SOP

Use when the customer paid before or at the moment the sale was made. Examples:

  • Someone bought a candle at a craft fair (paid cash)
  • An online sale through Stripe/Square
  • A workshop attendee paid via Venmo before the class

If they'll pay later, use SOP 1 (Invoice) instead.

Step-by-step

  1. Click + New.
  2. Under "Customers," click Sales receipt.
  3. Pick the Customer. (For one-off retail sales, your client may use a generic name like "Walk-in Customer.")
  4. Set the Sales receipt date to the day the sale happened.
  5. Pick the Payment method and (if a check) enter the reference number.
  6. In Deposit to, choose the account where the money went.
  7. Add line items: Product/Service, Description, Qty, Rate.
  8. Double-check the total. Does it match what the customer actually paid?
  9. Click Save and send if they want an email receipt, or Save and close if not.

Customer: Walk-in Customer

SOP 4 · Record an Expense

Every dollar your client spends has to be tracked — here's how to log it cleanly.

Objective

Record a purchase the business already paid for.

Goal

Categorize every expense correctly.

Description

Capture the date, vendor, amount, and category of any out-going dollar.

When to use this SOP

Use this when your client paid for something already (their card was swiped, money was sent, check was written). It's about recording history, not paying a future bill.

Examples: gas for the work van, lunch with a client, a $20 Canva subscription, office supplies from Staples.

Before you start, you need

  • A receipt (paper, email, or photo) — always file or attach this
  • Vendor name (who got paid)
  • Amount
  • Date
  • Payment method (which card or bank account)
  • The category the expense fits (Office Supplies? Meals? Travel?)

Step-by-step

  1. Click + New.
  2. Under "Vendors," click Expense.
  3. In Payee, pick the vendor. If they're not in the list, click "+ Add new."
  4. Pick the Payment account (which card or bank paid for this).
  5. Set the Payment date to the day the money actually left.
  6. Pick the Payment method (Credit card, debit, cash, etc.).
  7. (Optional) Type a Ref no. like a check number or transaction ID.
  8. Under Category, pick the right bucket from the Chart of Accounts (e.g., "Office Supplies," "Software," "Meals & Entertainment").
  9. Type a Description — your future self will thank you. "Staples - printer ink and paper" is way better than "stuff."
  10. Enter the Amount.
  11. Click the paperclip icon at the bottom to attach the receipt image. Always do this. Your accountant will love you.
  12. Click Save and close.

SOP 5 · Enter & Pay a Bill

For when a vendor sends an invoice your client will pay later.

Objective

Track money the business will owe and pay it on time.

Goal

Enter the bill today, pay it when it's due.

Description

Two steps: enter the bill, then later record the payment against it.

When to use this SOP

Use when a vendor sends a bill with a due date in the future. Examples:

  • The printer sent an invoice for $500, due in 30 days
  • The web developer billed $2,000, payable next month
  • The landlord sent the May rent bill, due June 1

If your client already paid for it, that's an Expense (SOP 4), not a Bill.

Part 1 · Enter the bill

  1. Click + New.
  2. Under "Vendors," click Bill.
  3. Pick the Vendor.
  4. Set the Bill date (the date on the vendor's invoice) and Due date (when they expect to be paid).
  5. Type the Bill no. from the vendor's invoice.
  6. Under Category details, pick the right category, type a description, and enter the amount.
  7. Attach the bill PDF using the paperclip icon.
  8. Click Save and close. The bill is now in the system as money owed.

Part 2 · Pay the bill (when it's time)

  1. Click + New.
  2. Under "Vendors," click Pay bills.
  3. Pick the Payment account (which bank account the money is coming from).
  4. Set the Payment date.
  5. Check the box next to the bill(s) you're paying.
  6. Confirm the amount in the Payment column.
  7. Click Save and close (or Save and print if mailing a paper check).

SOP 6 · Add a New Customer or Vendor

Two lookalike processes, one quick lesson.

Objective

Build clean customer and vendor records.

Goal

Add either one in under a minute.

Description

Capture name, contact info, and key details.

Adding a new customer

  1. From the left sidebar, click Sales → Customers (or just Customers).
  2. Click the green New customer button (top right).
  3. Fill in Customer display name — this is how they appear in lists. Use a clear name like "Beta Co." not "Mark from Beta."
  4. Add their Company name, Email, and Phone.
  5. Add the Billing address — this prints on invoices.
  6. Under Payments, set default terms (Net 30 is standard).
  7. Click Save.

Adding a new vendor

  1. From the left sidebar, click Expenses → Vendors.
  2. Click the green New vendor button.
  3. Fill in Vendor display name, Company name, Email, Phone.
  4. If this vendor is an independent contractor (not a corporation), check Track payments for 1099 — this matters at tax time.
  5. Add their Address if mailing checks.
  6. Click Save.

SOP 7 · Categorize the Bank Feed

QuickBooks pulls bank transactions in automatically. Your job is to sort them into the right category.

Objective

Sort each bank transaction into the right account.

Goal

Clear the "For Review" list every week.

Description

Decide what each transaction is, then accept or match it.

What is the bank feed?

When your client links their bank account to QuickBooks, every transaction from that bank shows up in a list called the Banking feed. They appear "For Review" — meaning QuickBooks doesn't know what they are yet. Your job is to tell it.

The three actions for each transaction

ActionWhen to use it
MatchQuickBooks already has this transaction (you entered an expense or received a payment). Just click Match to link them.
AddThis is new — pick a category and click Add to record it as a fresh expense or income.
View / Find MatchQuickBooks isn't sure. You manually pick the matching transaction (or transactions).

Step-by-step

  1. From the left sidebar, click Banking (or Transactions → Banking).
  2. Pick the bank account at the top.
  3. You'll see a list of transactions under the "For Review" tab.
  4. For each row, look at the description and amount. Ask yourself: "Is this already in QuickBooks?"
  5. If YES (e.g., you already entered the Staples expense) → click Match.
  6. If NO → pick a Vendor, pick a Category, then click Add.
  7. Move to the next row. Repeat until the list is empty.
  8. If you're stuck on one, leave it. Don't guess. Ask your client.
Banking · For Review (12)
05/15 · STARBUCKS #4421 · -$8.75 · guess: Meals?   Add Match
05/14 · STAPLES.COM · -$87.42 · ✅ matches existing expense   Match
05/13 · Deposit BETA CO · +$750.00 · ✅ matches Invoice 1042   Match
05/12 · ADOBE INC · -$54.99 · guess: Software?   Add
The 80/20 rule: After a few weeks, QuickBooks learns your patterns and starts guessing the category for you. You just confirm. The bank feed gets faster every week.
Never click "Add" without choosing a category. If you do, QuickBooks dumps it into "Uncategorized Expense" — which is messy and confuses the accountant.

SOP 8 · Run Basic Reports

The "pretty summaries" your client wants to see every week or month.

Objective

Generate and share the three reports clients ask for most.

Goal

Pull and email any of them in under 5 minutes.

Description

P&L, Balance Sheet, and A/R Aging — what they mean and how to make them.

The three reports you'll be asked for

ReportWhat it showsWhen asked for
Profit & Loss (P&L)Money in minus money out = profit for a time period.Every month/quarter
Balance SheetWhat the business owns, what it owes, what's left.Monthly / year-end
A/R Aging SummaryWho owes the business money and how late they are.Weekly

How to run any report

  1. From the left sidebar, click Reports.
  2. At the top, you'll see "Favorites." Common reports live there.
  3. Click the report name (e.g., "Profit and Loss").
  4. At the top of the report, set the Report period — "This Month," "Last Quarter," "This Year," or custom dates.
  5. Click Run report.
  6. Use the buttons at the top right to Email, Print, or Export to Excel/PDF.

What each report tells you

Profit & Loss: The top section is income. The middle is expenses. The bottom shows the difference — that's your client's profit (or loss) for the period.

Profit & Loss · May 1–31, 2026

Final Checklist & Cheat Sheet

Print this. Pin it next to your monitor. You are officially QuickBooks-capable.

Your daily workflow (memorize this)

  1. Open QuickBooks. Glance at the Dashboard.
  2. Click Banking. Categorize new bank feed items.
  3. Check the Sales tab. Send any invoices your client asked for.
  4. Record any payments received.
  5. Enter any new expenses or bills.
  6. On Fridays: Run the P&L and A/R Aging. Email them to your client.

The decision flowchart (when in doubt)

  • Customer paying NOW? → Sales Receipt
  • Customer paying LATER? → Invoice
  • Customer paid an old invoice? → Receive Payment
  • Client paid for something already? → Expense
  • Vendor sent a bill due later? → Bill → later → Pay Bills
  • New transaction in the bank feed? → Match if it already exists in QB, or Add with a category.

The 7 commandments of being a great QuickBooks VA

  1. Never guess at a category. Ask the client or accountant.
  2. Always attach receipts. Use the paperclip icon.
  3. Search before adding a new customer or vendor. Avoid duplicates.
  4. Type meaningful descriptions. Future-you will be grateful.
  5. Don't touch the Chart of Accounts without permission.
  6. Run a P&L weekly to spot weird numbers early.
  7. Communicate over-communicate. "Hey, I wasn't sure about this $200 from Best Buy — what category?" is golden.

What to say when a client asks "Do you know QuickBooks?"

"Yes — I'm comfortable with the daily operations: creating and sending invoices, recording customer payments, entering expenses and bills, categorizing transactions in the bank feed, and pulling reports like the Profit & Loss, Balance Sheet, and A/R Aging. I always check with the accountant before doing anything with the Chart of Accounts or journal entries."

That's a real, professional answer. It tells the truth, sets expectations, and signals that you know your limits — which clients love.
